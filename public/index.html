Module 01

Grammar

Build flexible sentence control so you can describe time, possibility, cause, and detail with confidence.

12 min focus B1 -> B2 accuracy B2 -> C1 range Best for writing and speaking
Target outcomes: choose accurate structures faster, avoid fossilized errors, and write with more variety.

Theory: Tenses are not only about time. They also show completion, duration, and relevance to the present.

Example: "I have worked on my pronunciation for three months, and I am starting to hear the difference."

Common mistake: Using present simple instead of present perfect for unfinished time periods.

Study advice: Compare one event in present simple, past simple, and present perfect to feel the change in meaning.

Theory: Modals express obligation, possibility, deduction, and advice. Small shifts create different levels of certainty.

Example: "She must be tired after teaching all day, but she might still join the meeting later."

Common mistake: Treating must, have to, and should as interchangeable.

Study advice: Group modals by function, not alphabetically.

Theory: Conditionals connect situations with consequences and help you discuss reality, habit, or imagination.

Example: "If I had recorded myself earlier, I would have noticed my pronunciation problems sooner."

Common mistake: Mixing time references without intention.

Study advice: Rewrite the same idea as a real condition, an unlikely condition, and a past regret.

Theory: These structures help you sound more formal and connect information efficiently in longer sentences.

Example: "The article, which was published last week, was shared by several teachers."

Common mistake: Overusing full stops instead of combining related ideas.

Study advice: Take simple sentences from news headlines and combine them into one richer sentence.

Finished past: "I studied English for three years at school." The period is complete.

Present result: "I have studied enough to join the advanced group." The result matters now.

Ongoing activity: "I have been studying since January." The duration or activity is emphasized.

C1 upgrade: Choose a tense from the meaning you want to express, not from a memorized time word.

Recall: Explain when present perfect is better than past simple without looking at the theory.

Produce: Write three connected sentences about your learning: one with a modal, one conditional, and one relative clause.

Self-check: Can you explain why each structure fits the meaning? If not, reopen only that topic.

Module 02

Vocabulary

Grow beyond isolated words and start using phrases, patterns, and register more naturally.

10 min focus B1 -> B2 natural phrasing B2 -> C1 precision Best for essays and conversation
Target outcomes: sound more precise, reduce translation habits, and expand your expressive range.

Theory: Strong English uses natural word partnerships, not just correct individual words.

Example: "Make progress," "take responsibility," and "raise awareness" sound more natural than literal translations.

Common mistake: Learning words alone and then combining them unnaturally.

Study advice: Save new vocabulary as chunks with a full example sentence.

Theory: Prefixes and suffixes help you shift meaning and word class without memorizing every form separately.

Example: "Decide," "decision," "decisive," and "indecisive" belong to one useful family.

Common mistake: Using the right root but the wrong form in exam tasks or essays.

Study advice: Build small word-family maps around common academic roots.

Theory: Range comes from knowing when informal, neutral, and formal language fits the context.

Example: "Find out" works in speech, while "determine" may fit a formal report better.

Common mistake: Using advanced words everywhere, even when they sound unnatural.

Study advice: Sort new phrases into speaking, general writing, and formal writing.

Informal: "Can you look into this problem?" Natural with colleagues or friends.

Neutral: "Could you investigate this issue?" Suitable in most professional contexts.

Formal: "We would appreciate a review of this matter." Diplomatic and less direct.

C1 upgrade: Advanced vocabulary means choosing the best fit, not always choosing the most formal option.

Recall: Choose five useful words and say the collocation or word family for each one.

Produce: Write a formal and an informal sentence that communicate the same idea.

Self-check: Have you learned reusable chunks, or only isolated definitions?

Module 03

Reading

Read more strategically by learning what to notice first and how to locate evidence faster.

12 min focus B1 -> B2 reading speed B2 -> C1 inference Best for exam-style texts
Target outcomes: improve speed, understand structure, and make stronger inferences from context.

Theory: Skimming gives you the general message; scanning helps you locate exact information quickly.

Example: Read the title, first sentence, and last sentence of each paragraph before checking details.

Common mistake: Reading every line with the same speed and attention.

Study advice: Set a short timer and practice finding names, dates, or opinions first.

Theory: High-level reading often depends on implied meaning, tone, and paragraph function.

Example: A contrast marker like "however" may signal the writer's real position more clearly than a topic sentence.

Common mistake: Looking only for repeated words instead of related ideas.

Study advice: Label each paragraph as example, argument, contrast, cause, or conclusion.

Theory: Pronouns, substitutions, and linking devices often reveal how ideas connect across sentences.

Example: In "This approach saves time," the word "this" points back to the strategy explained just before.

Common mistake: Missing the relationship between sentences because the exact noun is not repeated.

Study advice: Circle words like this, they, such, and former while reading longer texts.

Text: "Maya checked the departure board again and sighed. The platform was still empty."

Inference: Her train is probably delayed, although the text never states this directly.

Evidence: Checking "again," sighing, and the empty platform combine to support the conclusion.

C1 upgrade: Separate what the text proves from what is merely possible; strong inference needs several clues.

Recall: State the difference between skimming, scanning, and inference in one sentence each.

Produce: Open a short article and label each paragraph by function, not topic.

Self-check: Can you point to textual evidence for every conclusion you made?

Module 04

Writing

Develop clear organization and a more controlled tone across common task types.

15 min focus B1 -> B2 coherence B2 -> C1 tone control Best for essays and emails
Target outcomes: write more cohesive paragraphs, match tone to purpose, and produce cleaner formal texts.

Theory: Strong paragraphs usually develop one central idea with a clear opening, support, and a controlled finish.

Example: "One major benefit of studying abroad is independence. Students must manage their time, budget, and routines alone."

Common mistake: Adding many ideas to one paragraph without a clear focus.

Study advice: Highlight the topic sentence in each paragraph and check whether every supporting sentence belongs there.

Theory: Essays, emails, and reports need different levels of formality, directness, and structure.

Example: "I am writing to enquire about the course schedule" fits a formal email better than "I want to ask about the schedule."

Common mistake: Using spoken English in formal writing tasks.

Study advice: Keep model openings and closings for each text type.

Theory: Strong writing develops claims with a reason, an example, and a short explanation of why it matters.

Example: "Public transport should be cheaper because it reduces traffic. For instance, lower fares can encourage commuters to leave their cars at home."

Common mistake: Stating opinions in a list without developing them.

Study advice: After every main idea, ask: can I prove this with an example or consequence?

"Remote work can improve access to employment. People who live far from major cities, for example, can apply for roles that were previously unavailable to them. As a result, companies gain a wider range of candidates while workers gain more choice. However, this benefit depends on reliable internet access."

Why it works: The paragraph moves from claim to example, result, and qualification.

C1 upgrade: Add a limitation with however or provided that to make an argument more balanced.

Recall: Name the purpose, reader, and tone before choosing a task to write.

Produce: Draft one paragraph with a claim, reason, example, and closing sentence.

Self-check: Remove one sentence. If the paragraph becomes clearer, that sentence did not belong.

Module 05

Listening

Train your ear to follow meaning, not just individual words, especially in longer or faster input.

12 min focus B1 -> B2 gist first B2 -> C1 paraphrase tracking Best for lectures and interviews
Target outcomes: catch the main message faster, identify key details, and recognize paraphrased answers.

Theory: Good listeners separate the big picture from the supporting information.

Example: A speaker may mention travel, weather, and cost, but the main point could be a complaint about organization.

Common mistake: Panicking when one word is unfamiliar and missing the rest of the message.

Study advice: Listen once for the headline idea and once for supporting evidence.

Theory: Spoken English often signals direction with phrases like "the point is," "what changed was," or "in other words."

Example: The speaker says "it was overpriced," but the answer choice may say "it did not offer value for money."

Common mistake: Waiting for exact words from the question.

Study advice: Collect short phrases that introduce contrast, correction, and emphasis.

Theory: Speakers often reveal attitude through stress, hesitation, and contrast rather than direct statements.

Example: "It was interesting" can sound positive, polite, or doubtful depending on tone and context.

Common mistake: Focusing only on vocabulary and missing whether the speaker sounds enthusiastic, uncertain, or critical.

Study advice: Ask after each audio: what does the speaker want me to think or feel?

Question: Why did Lena reject the first proposal?

Audio: "It looked promising, but the costs would have been difficult to justify."

Answer: She thought it was too expensive. Difficult to justify the costs carries the same core meaning.

C1 upgrade: Predict synonyms before listening, then follow the speaker's idea instead of waiting for repeated words.

Recall: List three spoken signals for contrast, correction, or emphasis.

Produce: Listen to one minute of audio twice: summarize the gist first, then note three details.

Self-check: Did you identify the speaker's attitude from language, delivery, or both?

Module 06

Speaking

Speak with more control by organizing ideas quickly and supporting them with natural language.

10 min focus B1 -> B2 fluency building B2 -> C1 nuance Best for discussion and exams
Target outcomes: sound more fluent, compare ideas more clearly, and justify opinions with confidence.

Theory: Fluency improves when you connect ideas smoothly, not when you speak without pauses.

Example: "To begin with, I'd say online learning is flexible. At the same time, it can feel isolating."

Common mistake: Repeating the same filler phrase, such as "I think," in every sentence.

Study advice: Rotate opening phrases for opinions, contrast, and examples.

Theory: Advanced speaking often depends on how well you compare options and justify your view.

Example: "The first option seems more practical, whereas the second might be better for people who need flexibility."

Common mistake: Giving opinions without reasons or examples.

Study advice: Answer with a simple pattern: opinion, reason, example, final comment.

Theory: Good speakers pause strategically and use holding phrases to think while still sounding organized.

Example: "That is an interesting question. I would probably start by saying that..."

Common mistake: Filling every pause with repeated hesitation sounds or stopping completely.

Study advice: Prepare three or four neutral time-buying phrases and rotate them naturally.

Qualify: "That is true to some extent, although..."

Speculate: "One possible explanation might be that..."

Compare: "What both options have in common is..., whereas..."

Support: "A good example of what I mean is..."

C1 upgrade: Learn each phrase as a function and adapt the ending; avoid reciting a fixed answer.

Recall: Say three discourse markers and two natural time-buying phrases.

Produce: Record a one-minute answer using opinion, reason, example, and final comment.

Self-check: Listen once for clarity and once for variety. Improve only one issue per recording.
